PROJECT MANAGEMENT

Tier 3

A project is temporary and unique - it has a defined start and end, and produces something distinct from an organization's routine, ongoing operations. Managing one well requires balancing a well-known set of competing constraints.

1. What Defines a Project, and the Triple Constraint

The Project Management Institute (PMI), whose Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK) is one of the most widely referenced project management references globally, defines a project as a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result - distinguishing it from ongoing, repetitive operations. Rolling out a new member-registration system is a project; processing registrations afterward on an ongoing basis is operations.

The 'triple constraint' - scope, time, and cost - describes the three interdependent variables every project manager balances: increasing scope (what the project delivers) without adjusting time or cost is rarely realistic; compressing timeline typically requires either more resources (cost) or reduced scope. A commonly added fourth dimension, quality, sits at the center of all three - cutting corners on any of scope, time, or cost too aggressively tends to show up eventually as a quality problem.

A clearly defined scope - what the project will and will not deliver - is one of the most important documents a project can produce early on, because 'scope creep' (the gradual, often unapproved expansion of what a project is expected to deliver) is one of the most common reasons well-intentioned projects run over time and budget.

2. The Project Lifecycle and Common Methodologies

PMI describes five process groups that structure most projects regardless of methodology: initiating (defining the project and securing authorization), planning (developing the roadmap: scope, schedule, budget, resources), executing (carrying out the planned work), monitoring and controlling (tracking progress and managing changes), and closing (formally completing the project and capturing lessons learned).

'Waterfall' approaches to project management move through these stages largely sequentially, with each phase completed before the next begins - well suited to projects with stable, well-understood requirements from the outset, such as a regulatory-driven system change with fixed specifications. 'Agile' approaches instead work in short, iterative cycles, delivering working increments frequently and adjusting direction based on feedback - better suited to projects where requirements are expected to evolve as the work progresses, such as building a new digital member portal shaped by user testing.

Neither approach is universally superior; the right choice depends on how well-defined and stable a project's requirements are expected to be, and many organizations use a blend, applying the structure of waterfall planning to major milestones while running agile cycles for the detailed work within them.

3. Managing Risk and Stakeholders in Projects

Applying the risk management principles covered in the Risk Management category at the project level, a project risk register identifies what could delay, derail, or degrade a specific project, assigns an owner to each risk, and tracks mitigation. Project risk registers are typically reviewed far more frequently than organization-wide risk registers, since a project's risk profile can shift quickly within a compressed timeline.

Stakeholder mapping - identifying everyone with an interest in or influence over a project's outcome, and their relative level of interest and influence - helps a project team prioritize communication effort. A stakeholder with high influence but currently low engagement (such as a senior executive not yet closely following a project) often needs more proactive attention than one with high interest but limited influence, since their support or opposition, once activated, can significantly affect the project's trajectory.

A structured project close - formally confirming deliverables were met, releasing project resources, and documenting lessons learned for future projects - is frequently skipped once the practical work is finished, but skipping it means an organization repeatedly relearns the same lessons on each new project rather than building institutional project-management capability over time.
