Tier 3

Human resource practice covers the full arc of an employee's relationship with an institution — from how they are recruited, to how they are developed, to how fairly they are treated throughout their time there. Each stage shapes both individual experience and organizational capability.

1. The Employee Lifecycle as an HR Framework

HR practice is often organized around the employee lifecycle: recruitment and selection, onboarding, ongoing development, performance management (covered in its own category), and eventual exit — whether through retirement, resignation, or another outcome. Thinking in terms of this full cycle, rather than isolated HR activities, helps ensure that decisions made at one stage (such as how a role is recruited for) are consistent with expectations set at later stages (such as how that role is later evaluated).

Onboarding — the process of integrating a new employee into an organization — is frequently under-invested relative to its impact. Research on employee retention consistently finds that a structured, well-supported onboarding period in the first weeks and months significantly affects whether a new hire becomes a fully productive, engaged team member or struggles and disengages early, regardless of how strong their original qualifications were.

Exit processes, similarly, are often treated as administrative afterthoughts, but structured exit interviews and clean offboarding can surface valuable, otherwise-hard-to-obtain feedback about systemic issues — since departing staff are often more candid than current staff about problems they experienced.

2. Fair and Inclusive Recruitment

Unstructured interviews — where interviewers ask whatever questions occur to them in the moment — are consistently found in research to be less predictive of actual job performance and more susceptible to unconscious bias than structured interviews, where every candidate is asked the same core set of job-relevant questions and evaluated against the same defined criteria.

Unconscious bias — systematic, unintentional favoring or disfavoring of certain candidates based on factors unrelated to job performance — can influence recruitment decisions even among interviewers who consciously value fairness. Structured processes, diverse interview panels, and criteria agreed before interviews begin (rather than adjusted afterward to justify a preferred outcome) are common practical safeguards.

Equal opportunity in recruitment means evaluating candidates on job-relevant criteria consistently applied, rather than on factors unrelated to the ability to perform the role. This is both good practice and, in most jurisdictions, a legal requirement — but treating it purely as legal compliance misses its practical value: fairer recruitment processes also tend to produce better hiring decisions by widening genuine consideration of qualified candidates.

3. Employee Development and Retention

Career pathing — giving employees visibility into plausible future roles and the skills or experience needed to reach them — is a consistently cited driver of engagement and retention, particularly for capable staff who might otherwise assume, in its absence, that growth is only available by leaving the organization.

A learning culture — where ongoing skill development is normalized and supported, not treated as an occasional, optional event — tends to correlate with both stronger individual performance and higher retention, since employees who feel they are growing professionally are, on average, less likely to look elsewhere.

Employee engagement — the degree to which staff feel invested in and connected to their work and organization — is distinct from simple satisfaction; an employee can be satisfied (comfortable, no major complaints) without being genuinely engaged (motivated, discretionary effort, connected to organizational purpose). HR practice increasingly focuses on engagement specifically, because it correlates more strongly with performance and retention than satisfaction alone.
