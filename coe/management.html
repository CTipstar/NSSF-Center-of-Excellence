General

Management, as a discipline, is older and broader than any single management fad - its core functions and common failure modes have stayed remarkably consistent across a century of study.

1. The Four Classical Functions of Management

Early management theorist Henri Fayol proposed a framework, still widely taught today, describing management as consisting of four core functions: planning (deciding what needs to happen and how), organizing (arranging people and resources to make it happen), leading (motivating and directing people toward the plan), and controlling (monitoring progress and correcting course when needed).

These functions operate as a repeating cycle rather than a one-time sequence. Controlling a process typically reveals information that feeds back into planning for the next cycle - a manager who plans once and never revisits that plan in light of what controlling reveals is only performing part of the role.

Peter Drucker, a highly influential management theorist writing decades after Fayol, added an emphasis on managing for results and treating knowledge workers differently from industrial labor - recognizing that motivating and organizing people whose main output is judgment and decision-making requires different tools than managing repetitive physical production.

2. Management Styles and When to Use Them

Management style research commonly distinguishes autocratic (the manager makes decisions with little input from the team), democratic (the manager actively seeks and incorporates team input before deciding), and laissez-faire (the manager sets broad direction and leaves most decisions to the team) approaches, among others.

No single style is correct in all situations. An autocratic style may be appropriate during a genuine crisis requiring fast, decisive action; a laissez-faire style may work well with a highly experienced, self-directed team; a democratic style often suits situations where buy-in and diverse input meaningfully improve the decision. This echoes the situational leadership concept covered in the Leadership & People Development category, applied specifically to management decision-making rather than leadership influence more broadly.

A common mistake is defaulting to one style regardless of context - either an inflexibly autocratic manager who never invites input even when it would improve decisions, or a conflict-avoidant manager who defaults to laissez-faire even when a team clearly needs more direction.

3. Managing Teams and Workflows

'Span of control' refers to the number of people a single manager directly oversees. A span that is too wide can leave a manager unable to give each person meaningful attention; a span that is too narrow can create unnecessary layers of management and slow decision-making. There is no universal correct number - it depends on how standardized the work is and how much individual judgment each role requires.

Delegation, also discussed in the Leadership & People Development category, is a core management workflow tool: assigning specific responsibility and the authority to match, with clear expectations about outcomes and check-in points, rather than either micromanaging every detail or handing off a task with no follow-up at all.

Organizational structure - how reporting lines and decision rights are arranged - shapes how smoothly work actually flows in practice. A structure with unclear ownership of a given task often produces delays and finger-pointing even when every individual involved is competent and well-intentioned, because the problem is structural rather than personal.

4. Common Management Pitfalls

Micromanagement - closely controlling and reviewing every detail of a subordinate's work rather than setting clear expectations and trusting execution - tends to erode trust and initiative over time, even when the underlying intent is a reasonable concern for quality.

The 'Peter Principle' is an observation that people in a hierarchy tend to be promoted based on performance in their current role until they reach a role where they are no longer competent, at which point they stop being promoted further - meaning many positions end up filled by people who were excellent at their previous job but are not necessarily well-suited to their current one. It is a caution against assuming strong performance in one role automatically predicts strong performance in a different, more senior one.

Poor delegation - either failing to delegate at all, or delegating a task without the authority needed to actually complete it - is a recurring cause of both manager burnout and staff frustration, and is generally more a management skill gap than a reflection of the people involved.
