LEADERSHIP & PEOPLE DEVELOPMENT

Tier 3

Leadership is frequently conflated with formal authority, but influence over people's motivation and direction can be exercised - or fail to be exercised - at any level of an organization, regardless of job title.

1. What Leadership Is, and How It Differs From Management

A widely used distinction, associated with management scholar John Kotter, separates leadership from management by function rather than seniority: management is primarily about coping with complexity - planning, budgeting, organizing, controlling, to produce order and predictability - while leadership is primarily about coping with change - setting direction, aligning people around it, and motivating them to move toward it, especially amid uncertainty. Most roles require both functions in some balance, and the balance often shifts depending on circumstances.

Leadership, in this sense, is not confined to formal position. A staff member without any direct reports can exercise real leadership by proposing a better way of working, building consensus among peers, or taking initiative to solve a problem no one has formally assigned to them. Conversely, someone with a formal management title but limited actual influence over how their team thinks and acts is exercising authority without necessarily exercising leadership in this fuller sense.

Influence, in leadership terms, is generally built through demonstrated competence, consistency between stated values and observed behavior, and genuine concern for the people being led - none of which are automatically granted by a title, and all of which can, in principle, be developed deliberately over time.

2. Adapting Leadership Style to Context

Situational leadership theory, developed by Paul Hersey and Ken Blanchard, holds that effective leadership style should adapt to the readiness - competence and confidence - of the person being led for a given task, rather than applying one fixed style universally. A highly directive style may suit a new employee learning an unfamiliar process, while the same style applied to an experienced, confident staff member on familiar territory can feel micromanaging and demotivating.

Servant leadership, a concept introduced by Robert Greenleaf, reframes the leader's primary role as serving the needs of the team - removing obstacles, providing resources, developing capability - rather than positioning the team as primarily there to serve the leader's agenda. This is not the same as being passive or avoiding difficult decisions; a servant leader can still set clear direction and hold people accountable, but does so in service of the team's success rather than personal status.

No single leadership style is universally correct. Effective leaders tend to have a broader repertoire of approaches and the judgment to select an appropriate one for a given person, task, and moment, rather than relying on a single default style regardless of context.

3. Developing the People Around You

Delegation - assigning meaningful responsibility and authority to others, not just tasks - is one of the most consistently underused leadership tools, often because it feels slower in the short term than doing something yourself. Its longer-term payoff is twofold: it builds the capability of the person delegated to, and it frees the leader's own time for work only they can do, which compounds over time in a way that retaining every task personally does not.

Coaching, introduced in the Performance Management category, is a core leadership skill in its own right: asking questions that help someone work through a problem and arrive at their own solution generally builds more lasting capability than simply providing the answer, even though it takes more patience in the moment.

Succession planning - deliberately identifying and developing potential future leaders within an organization, rather than only reacting when a key role unexpectedly becomes vacant - is a leadership responsibility that is easy to defer indefinitely because it rarely feels urgent, right up until a departure makes the lack of a plan suddenly very costly.
